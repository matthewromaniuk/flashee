Photosynthesis is the biological process in which plants, algae, and some bacteria convert light energy from the sun into chemical energy stored in glucose. This process occurs primarily in the leaves of plants and is essential for the survival of most life on Earth. Photosynthesis is responsible for producing the oxygen that many organisms need to survive.

The photosynthetic process takes place in two main stages: the light-dependent reactions and the Calvin cycle, also known as the light-independent reactions or the dark reactions. The light-dependent reactions occur in the thylakoid membranes of the chloroplast and require sunlight to proceed. During this stage, light energy is captured by chlorophyll and other light-absorbing pigments, which excite electrons to higher energy states.

Chlorophyll is the primary pigment responsible for capturing light energy in photosynthesis. It absorbs light most strongly in the blue and red regions of the electromagnetic spectrum. The most common form of chlorophyll, chlorophyll a, gives plants their characteristic green color. Chlorophyll b is also present in plant leaves and helps expand the range of light wavelengths that can be used.

During the light-dependent reactions, excited electrons move through an electron transport chain, generating energy in the form of ATP and NADPH. Water molecules are split to provide electrons, a process called photolysis. Oxygen is released as a byproduct of this water-splitting process. This oxygen is released into the atmosphere as waste from the plant's perspective, but it is essential for aerobic respiration in animals.

The Calvin cycle is the second stage of photosynthesis and occurs in the stroma of the chloroplast. This stage does not directly require light, though it depends on the ATP and NADPH produced in the light-dependent reactions. The Calvin cycle uses carbon dioxide from the atmosphere to synthesize glucose. This process involves three main steps: carbon fixation, reduction, and regeneration of the starting compound.

Carbon fixation is the first step of the Calvin cycle, during which the enzyme RuBisCO (ribulose-1,5-bisphosphate carboxylase/oxygenase) attaches carbon dioxide to a five-carbon sugar called ribulose bisphosphate. This produces an unstable six-carbon compound that immediately breaks down into two three-carbon molecules called 3-phosphoglycerate. RuBisCO is considered the most abundant protein on Earth.

The rate of photosynthesis is influenced by several environmental factors including light intensity, carbon dioxide concentration, temperature, and water availability. As light intensity increases, the rate of photosynthesis increases until it reaches saturation, at which point other factors become limiting. Similarly, increasing carbon dioxide concentration can enhance photosynthesis up to a point where another factor becomes limiting.

Different types of plants have evolved different photosynthetic strategies to optimize energy capture in their specific environments. C3 plants, which include most terrestrial plants, perform the standard photosynthetic cycle described above. C4 plants, such as corn and sugarcane, have evolved a modified photosynthetic pathway that concentrates carbon dioxide around RuBisCO, making photosynthesis more efficient. CAM plants, such as cacti, open their stomata at night to collect carbon dioxide and perform photosynthesis during the day with reduced water loss.
